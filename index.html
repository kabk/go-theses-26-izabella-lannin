Chapter 1

The Royal Seaside Seal of Approval

The year is 1789 and “God Save the King!” sings the crowd on the streets; Union Jacks decorate the windows, and everyone is dressed in their royal best. They cheer, for King George III and his royal party have finally arrived in their town of Weymouth. Starting early in his life, the King had long been known to have suffered bouts of “madness” (bouts, we now know were catalysed by a rare blood condition), and in a bid to help him, the doctors had prescribed a prolonged visit to the seaside for some clean air and salt water.

Miraculously, his symptoms improved ten-fold, and although it would not cure him in the long run, he had been temporarily aided and he returned to the seaside almost every year until the start of the Napoleonic wars in 1805, when the threat of French invasion was too great for the coast to remain unprotected. Although the Napoleonic wars would stall the access to seasides until 1815, once peace was secured, they soon regained [currency/social standing] and the King would eventually buy a holiday home on the seafront.

Consequently, the royal seal of seaside approval meant that the coast was now increasingly open to higher class Georgians; as a result other towns such as Bognor Regis, Scarborough, Margate or Brighton relished in new-founded popularity and responded by opening spas or resorts of their own; their popularity marked by a period of time in which of very few other medical alternatives or opportunities were available. Many of these seaside resorts had their advent in existing fishing villages; the wealthy tourists finding entertainment in the local populations, and often partaking in boat excursions provided by the fishermen, or indulging in the fresh seafood. The two populations, though still heavily divided by class, coexisted together, and set the precedent.

The seaside’s early nineteenth-century popularity is also honoured by prominent authors of the time; Jane Austen often sent her most memorable characters to promenade along the shore. In Pride & Prejudice, Lydia and Mr. Wickham run away to elope in Brighton, Emma’s Jane Fairfax and Frank Churchill become engaged in Weymouth, and in Persuasion, Austen has her characters go to Lyme. However as the eighteen-forties rolled around, a second phase of expansion was marked with the introduction of the steam railway.
